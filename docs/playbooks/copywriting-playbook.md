# RecruiterOS · Copy & Persuasion Playbook

**Baseline:** Edward L. Bernays, *Propaganda* (1928) — the foundational text on
influencing mass behavior. This playbook translates its principles into concrete rules
for every word RecruiterOS publishes: the **website** and the **correspondence** (the
recruiter→prospect and recruiter→candidate messages the platform sends).

**The one idea that governs everything (Bernays' "interest coincidence"):**
> "The propagandist must... bring his message into coincidence with the audience's
> existing desires and interests."

We never interrupt. We arrive with proof we understand the moment. That is not a slogan —
it is literally the product: a **signal** gives us the context, context creates
**relevance**, relevance earns the **response**. Persuasion here is honest because it is
grounded in a real, observed event, not a manufactured one.

---

## The ten techniques, applied

Each principle below is stated in Bernays' terms, then mapped to **website copy** and to
**outreach correspondence**, with a concrete before/after.

### 1. Interest coincidence — speak to what they already want
- **Bernays:** align the message with the audience's existing desires.
- **Website:** lead every page with the reader's outcome, not our feature.
- **Correspondence:** open on *their* situation (the signal), never on our ask.

> ✕ Bad: "We're a recruiting platform with AI sourcing and multichannel outreach."
> ✓ Good: "You just posted three engineering leadership roles in two weeks. Here's how
> to fill them before the backlog becomes attrition."

### 2. Associative connection — borrow credibility by association
- **Bernays:** "link your idea to admired figures, institutions, or social values."
- **Website:** anchor claims to recognizable references (the firms, the stacks, the
  outcomes the reader respects). Show, don't assert.
- **Correspondence:** name the specific, admirable thing they did. Recognition before
  pitch.

> ✓ "Your work on the Trade Republic order flow is some of the cleanest trading UI on the
> web." (specific, true, flattering — earns the next sentence)

### 3. Hidden-motivation appeal — sell the deeper desire, not the spec
- **Bernays:** address the unconscious want behind the surface want.
- **Website:** under "find candidates" is *be first, look effortless, hit the number*.
  Write to that.
- **Correspondence:** under "a new job" is *recognition, momentum, not being stuck*.

> ✓ "A role where you set the architecture from day one" beats "a senior React role."
> The first sells status and autonomy; the second sells a task.

### 4. Leader-based influence — move the few who move the many
- **Bernays:** "if you influence the leaders... you influence the group they sway."
- **Website:** target the decision-maker's worldview (the founder, the VP, the agency
  owner), not the end-user clerk.
- **Correspondence:** prioritize hiring managers and function leads; one yes cascades to
  their team and their vendors.

### 5. Circumstance creation — make the message feel like the moment, not an ad
- **Bernays:** manufacture the context so the message reads as natural demand.
- **Website:** frame timing as *theirs*. "The week the round is announced," "the 90-day
  window after a new VP starts."
- **Correspondence:** the signal IS the circumstance. Let the event do the asking.

> ✓ "Most teams hit capacity during a market expansion — curious how you're handling
> talent right now." The expansion (real) makes the outreach feel inevitable, not random.

### 6. Intermediary / trusted-authority use — let a third party vouch
- **Bernays:** work through doctors, experts, the trusted voice — not direct claims.
- **Website:** proof via numbers, named integrations, and customer outcomes rather than
  adjectives. Let the data be the authority.
- **Correspondence:** reference mutual connections, the candidate's own peers, the
  market ("six of your team updated to 'open to work' this week").

### 7. Multi-channel saturation — one message, many surfaces
- **Bernays:** reach the audience through every channel at once.
- **Website + Correspondence:** the same signal-grounded story shows up as the email,
  the LinkedIn note, the SMS, and the voice note — varied in form, identical in premise.
  This is the Outreach sequence: email → LinkedIn → SMS → breakup, each a different angle
  on the *same* coincidence of interest.

### 8. Cliché & symbol manipulation — compress meaning into a charged phrase
- **Bernays:** "by playing upon a cliché... the propagandist can swing group emotions."
- **Website:** own a few sticky, emotionally loaded phrases and repeat them: *"Stop
  interrupting. Start arriving on time." "Be the first relevant message they read."*
- **Correspondence:** one vivid line beats a paragraph. "Great talent hits the market in
  batches — and the batch just dropped."
- **Rule:** coin the phrase once, then reuse it across the whole site and sequence so it
  becomes ours.

### 9. Stereotype leverage — ride the belief they already hold
- **Bernays:** use existing mental patterns instead of fighting them.
- **Website:** recruiters already believe cold outreach is dying and timing wins. Agree
  loudly, then position RecruiterOS as the obvious answer to a belief they already hold.
- **Correspondence:** candidates already believe the 3–4 year mark is "time to look."
  Name it; don't argue it.

### 10. Group-formation activation — speak to the identity, not just the person
- **Bernays:** target existing social structures and identities.
- **Website:** write to "modern recruiters," "agency owners," "founders building their
  first team" — name the tribe so the reader self-selects in.
- **Correspondence:** "the senior engineer who's outgrown maintenance work" is an
  identity; speak to it and the individual feels seen.

## The website copy system

**Page formula (every page):**
1. **Headline** = their outcome + a charged phrase (techniques 1, 8).
2. **Subhead** = the hidden desire made explicit (technique 3).
3. **Proof band** = numbers / names / integrations as the authority (technique 6).
4. **Mechanism** = how it works, framed as *their* timing (technique 5).
5. **CTA** = a low-friction next step phrased as their gain, not our demand.

**Voice rules (carry the existing brand standards):**
- No em/en dashes — use commas. All money in USD ($). RecruiterOS wordmark, no "R" badge.
- Second person ("you"), present tense, active voice.
- One idea per sentence. Cut every adjective that a number could replace.
- Each page owns ONE charged phrase and repeats it; the site shares a small vocabulary of
  them (technique 8).

**House phrases (the cliché bank — reuse verbatim):**
- "Stop interrupting. Start arriving on time."
- "Be the first relevant message they read."
- "Signal → Context → Relevance → Response."
- "Context creates relevance. Relevance creates response."
- "Great talent hits the market in batches."

## The correspondence system

Every message is the same four-beat arc — the rapport-first rungs already enforced in the
LinkedIn Engine (`recognize → relate → invite → pitch → release`). Bernays' techniques map
onto the beats:

| Beat | Technique | What it does |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **Recognize** | 2 Associative, 6 Authority | Name the specific, admirable thing. Earn the read. |
| **Relate** | 5 Circumstance, 9 Stereotype | State the signal as their moment. Make outreach feel inevitable. |
| **Invite** | 1 Interest coincidence, 3 Hidden motive | Offer the deeper want (status, momentum, relief), low friction. |
| **Pitch** | 3 Hidden motive, 8 Charged phrase | The concrete ask, one vivid line. |
| **Release** | 5 Circumstance | The honest exit that keeps the door open. |

**Template — recruiting (candidate), email touch #1:**
> Subject: the {company} {event}, and your {their-best-work}
>
> Hi {first},
>
> Saw {company} {signal-event, stated as fact}. (recognize + circumstance)
>
> You've spent {tenure} owning {specific work} — {genuine, specific compliment}. (associate)
>
> Teams usually hit capacity right when this happens, so people who can {their strength}
> get pulled in ten directions. (stereotype they already believe)
>
> I'm working with a team building {role framed as the hidden desire — autonomy/status},
> fully remote, ${comp}. You'd {the deeper want}, not {the thing they're tired of}. (hidden motive)
>
> Worth a 15-minute call this week? (low-friction invite)
>
> {sender}

**Template — business development (hiring manager), email touch #1:**
> Subject: {company}'s {signal} — the part most teams underestimate
>
> Hi {first},
>
> Noticed {company} {signal: raised / posted N roles / opened a {city} hub}. (circumstance)
>
> The pattern we see: {admired peer move / market truth} — the senior team that's holding
> it together quietly hits capacity first. (stereotype + authority)
>
> We help teams in exactly this window fill {function} roles before the backlog turns into
> attrition. {one proof number}. (interest coincidence + authority)
>
> Open to a quick look at how we'd approach {company}'s {function} hiring? (invite)
>
> {sender}

**Message rules:**
- The signal is the subject line. No signal, no send (it's an interruption).
- One compliment, and it must be specific and true (technique 2 dies if generic).
- Sell the deeper want, name the concrete spec once (technique 3 + 8).
- Never more than one ask. The breakup keeps the circumstance open, never guilt-trips.
- Same premise across email / LinkedIn / SMS / voice — different length, one story (7).

## The ethics line (non-negotiable)

Bernays' methods are powerful enough to be misused; ours stay honest by one rule:
**every persuasive move must be anchored to a real, observed signal and a true claim.**
We use circumstance creation by *reporting a real event*, not inventing one. We use
associative connection with *genuine* recognition. The persuasion is in the framing and
timing — never in the facts. That is also what makes it work: relevance only converts when
it's real.
